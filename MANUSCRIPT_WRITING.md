## 1. Theoretical framework

We frame government procurement of dedicated legal counsel as a state-capacity intervention that operates simultaneously on (i) the government's own representation in administrative litigation, (ii) the political-legal exposure that potential plaintiffs anticipate when deciding whether to bring an administrative suit, and (iii) the contracted firm's broader civil-litigation portfolio. We embed the empirical specification in three intersecting strands of literature: the authoritarian-legality strand on whether litigation against the state can constrain bureaucratic behavior under a single-party regime (Stern 2013; He and Su 2013; Liebman 2014); the state-capacity strand on whether bureaucratic professionalization translates into measurable performance gains (Wang 2015; Cai and Liu 2019; Liu, Wang, and Lyu 2023); and the bureaucratic-private interface strand on how state contracting reshapes the behavior of the firms it hires (Ang 2020). Existing work has documented administrative-litigation reform episodes and the within-court win-rate gradient, but has not isolated procurement of legal counsel as a distinct intervention nor traced its consequences from the government party through to the contracted law firm's private-client work. The unit-of-analysis structure of our four panels — city, administrative case, civil document, and firm — is not a parallel set of robustness checks but a layered causal chain: a city-level policy alters the government's representation quality in individual administrative cases (the inner-court margin), and the same policy reallocates the contracted firm's lawyer-hours and client mix in non-government civil cases (the firm-level externality margin).

Concretely, three competing hypotheses can each rationalize a portion of the reduced-form pattern, and the four-layer design lets us test them against one another rather than ask whether the policy "works."

* **H1. State-capacity / quality channel.** Procurement raises the average litigation skill on the government side, so the conditional probability of a government victory rises and the probability of an appeal falls; once a city procures, a constant pool of plaintiffs sees a more capable defendant. This hypothesis predicts that the government-win effect should appear *both* in the unconditional administrative panel and in the merits-decided sub-sample (since the change is in case-by-case representation quality, not selection at the docket margin).

* **H2. Selection / chilling channel.** Procurement is publicly observable and changes the perceived odds of bringing the government to court; potential plaintiffs withdraw or never file, so the government's apparent win rate rises mechanically because the marginal case is no longer brought. This hypothesis predicts that the headline government-win and appeal-rate effects should weaken (and the case-count effect should strengthen) as one moves from the unconditional sample to the merits-decided sub-sample, and that the composition of plaintiffs (entity vs individual; local vs non-local) should shift toward those least sensitive to political signals.

* **H3. Firm-capture channel.** Procurement re-routes the contracted firm's lawyer-hours and reputational alignment toward a "government-counsel" identity; the firm's civil-case style and client mix shift away from individual plaintiffs toward enterprise clients, the share of legal-reasoning text falls, and the firm's measurable civil-case outcomes change even on cases that have nothing to do with the procuring city's government. This hypothesis predicts that the document-level Winner $\times$ Post effects should appear in pure-private civil cases (no organizational party on either side), and that the firm-year stacked effects on enterprise share and civil-case outcomes should be of the same sign and order of magnitude as on cases with organizational parties.

The conceptual flow can be summarised as:

```
Procurement of legal counsel  ─►  (a) Government representation quality  ─►  Admin outcomes  (H1)
                              ─►  (b) Plaintiffs' filing decisions       ─►  Admin docket    (H2)
                              ─►  (c) Contracted firm reallocation       ─►  Civil docs / firm-year outcomes  (H3)
```

The four estimating panels map onto these three arrows: city-year and admin-case panels speak to (a) and (b); the document-level and firm-year panels speak to (c).

## 2. Substantive interpretation of the city-year headline effects

The point estimates from the calibrated city-year main table (`output/tables/city_year_cs_twfe_main_table.tex`) translate as follows. We use the TWFE coefficient as the linear summary; the CS estimator yields larger absolute magnitudes throughout, so the TWFE figures are conservative.

**Government win rate, +3.6 pp (TWFE; +4.9 pp CS).** In the calibrated sample, the average treated city has 282 cities $\times$ 7 post-procurement years $\times$ 380 administrative cases per city-year on the procurement-adopting side; restricted to the 712 treated city-years, the mean caseload is 380 cases per cell. Multiplying the 3.6 pp TWFE coefficient by the per-cell caseload, procurement converts roughly **13.8 administrative cases per city-year from a non-government victory to a government victory** ([`output/tables/back_of_envelope_substantive.txt`](output/tables/back_of_envelope_substantive.txt)). Across all 712 treated city-years this implies on the order of **9,800 cases** that flip outcome relative to the never-treated counterfactual. Against the empirical pre-period distribution of city-year government-win rates (P25 = 0.65, P50 = 0.70, P75 = 0.76, P90 = 0.83), a +3.6 pp shift moves the median pre-procurement city-year up to roughly the 65th percentile. Procurement therefore does not push a low-performing city to the top of the distribution, but it shifts a typical city about half of the inter-quartile distance toward the upper end.

**Appeal rate, −6.2 pp (TWFE; −11.6 pp CS).** Procurement is associated with **about 24 fewer appeals per treated city-year** (and on the order of **17,000 fewer appeals over the treated post-period**). The pre-period appeal rate had P25 = 0.37, P50 = 0.45, and P75 = 0.52; a −6.2 pp shift moves the median pre-procurement city-year down to roughly the 30th percentile, i.e. close to the lower quartile of the never-treated distribution.

**Administrative case count, −23.1 cases per city-year (TWFE; −91.5 cases CS).** Procurement is associated with **23 fewer cases per treated city-year** in the TWFE specification, and roughly **16,000 fewer cases in total** over the treated post-period. Against the pre-period case-count distribution (P25 = 141, P50 = 229, P75 = 349, P90 = 475), the TWFE estimate moves the median pre-procurement city-year down to roughly the 45th percentile — a modest but visible contraction of the docket. The Callaway-Sant'Anna estimate is several times larger and would put the same median city near the 10th-15th percentile.

These three numbers are jointly consistent with both H1 and H2: cases get scarcer (consistent with chilling) *and* the conditional government win rate rises (consistent with quality). The auxiliary tests — selection vs quality (`output/tables/admin_mechanism_selection_vs_quality_table.tex`), plaintiff entry (`output/tables/admin_mechanism_plaintiff_entry_table.tex`), and political sensitivity (`output/tables/admin_mechanism_cause_sensitivity_table.tex`) — partition the reduced-form into the H1 and H2 components.

## 3. Reading the four-layer estimates as a sequence of hypothesis tests

* **H1 vs H2 at the administrative panel.** The selection-vs-quality table reports the LPM ATT on government_win across nested sub-samples: all cases, non-withdrawn cases, and merits-decided cases (`output/tables/admin_mechanism_selection_vs_quality_table.tex`). If H1 is the operative channel the coefficient should be similar across the three columns; if H2 is the operative channel the coefficient should attenuate as the sample is restricted to cases that survived withdrawal and reached merits. The plaintiff-entry table separates these two channels at the city-year level by tracking entity-vs-individual and local-vs-non-local plaintiff shares (`output/tables/admin_mechanism_plaintiff_entry_table.tex`); a flat conditional win rate combined with sharp shifts in plaintiff composition would push the interpretation toward H2. The cause-sensitivity table asks whether the procurement effect is concentrated in politically charged cause groups (`output/tables/admin_mechanism_cause_sensitivity_table.tex`); a much larger effect in expropriation and land-planning cases than in labor and permitting cases would corroborate the H1+H2 mixture against a pure quality story.

* **H3 at the document and firm panels.** The pure-private placebo is the cleanest H3 test: if the contracted firm's gain is portable across case types, then Winner $\times$ Post should appear on civil documents in which both parties are individuals (`output/tables/document_mechanism_pure_private_placebo_table.tex`). The reasoning-decomposition table separates whether the reasoning-share decline reflects shorter reasoning sections, longer documents, or both (`output/tables/document_mechanism_reasoning_decomposition_table.tex`); a fall in share driven primarily by longer non-reasoning sections would be more consistent with a stylistic / boilerplate substitution than with a pure quality decline. The firm-year stacked DID and the client-mix mechanism table, together with the by-attribute event studies (`output/figures/document_mechanism_event_study_by_*.pdf`), localize which lawyer subgroup drives the firm-level shift.

* **SUTVA / spillover.** The within-court spillover test asks whether loser firms in the same court as the winner display offsetting changes (`output/tables/firm_sutva_same_court_spillover_table.tex`); a negative coefficient on Loser-in-Winner-Court $\times$ Post would imply that the firm-level estimate over-states the policy effect because it absorbs business displaced from competitors. The neighbour-treated table runs the parallel test at the city level (`output/tables/city_sutva_neighbor_treated_table.tex`); a meaningful coefficient on share-neighbour-treated would indicate within-province spillovers. The carry-over / lag table asks whether the firm-year effect persists or is concentrated in the contract year (`output/tables/firm_sutva_carryover_lag_table.tex`); the fect period-wise ATT relative to exit and the carry-over test focus on the multi-contract cities where the contract eventually changes hands (`output/tables/firm_fect_carryover_exit_table.tex`, with figures `output/figures/firm_fect_exit_*.pdf`).

* **Estimator robustness.** The alternative-estimators table reports the city-year coefficient under TWFE, Callaway-Sant'Anna, Sun-Abraham, and Borusyak-Jaravel-Spiess imputation (`output/tables/city_year_alternative_estimators_appendix_table.tex`); the negative-weights diagnostic reports the share of negative TWFE weights (`output/tables/city_year_negative_weights_diagnostic_appendix_table.tex`); the Honest DID bounds report the identified sets at $M = \{0, 0.5, 1\} \times \max|\text{pre-coef}|$ (`output/tables/city_year_honest_did_appendix_table.tex`, figures `output/figures/honest_did_*.pdf`); the Bacon decomposition reports the weight share and average effect of each comparison type (`output/tables/city_year_bacon_decomposition_appendix_table.tex`, figure `output/figures/city_year_bacon_decomposition.pdf`). Inference is supplemented by city-level permutation and wild cluster bootstrap-$t$ p-values (`output/tables/city_year_randomization_inference_appendix_table.tex`, figure `output/figures/city_year_permutation_distribution.pdf`).

* **Multiple-testing across outcome families.** The Romano-Wolf step-down adjustment within each outcome family (3 city-year outcomes; 4 document-level outcomes; 5 firm-year outcomes) is reported in `output/tables/family_wise_pvalues_appendix_table.tex` alongside Bonferroni-Holm, so a reader can check that the headline coefficients survive family-wise inference rather than only marginal-test inference.

* **Selection of contractor firms.** The firm-level logit on procurement-winner selection (`output/tables/firm_winner_selection_logit_appendix_table.tex`) reports which pre-period firm characteristics predict winning the tender. The pseudo-$R^2$ and AUC speak directly to the audit's concern that winners are not a random subset of competing firms; the coefficient table tells the reader on which dimensions the eventual winners are pre-selected (firm size, civil-case experience, client mix), which in turn calibrates how much of the firm-year DID could plausibly reflect ex-ante selection rather than treatment.

## 4. Suggested wording for the manuscript

> *"Three competing hypotheses can each rationalize a positive coefficient on Treatment $\times$ Post in the city-year administrative panel. (H1) Procurement raises the government's representation quality, so the average case is decided more favorably to the state on the merits. (H2) Procurement signals the state's enhanced legal capacity to potential plaintiffs, who self-select out of the docket; the apparent gain in government win rate is then mechanical, driven by a shift in which cases are filed. (H3) The contracted firm reorients its private-client work toward enterprise clients and government-style legal reasoning, so part of the reduced-form gain reflects the firm's portable rents rather than the city's improved defense. We design our four-layer empirical strategy around the observable implications of these three hypotheses: H1 predicts that the government-win effect survives in the merits-decided sub-sample and is roughly homogeneous across politically sensitive and routine cause groups; H2 predicts that the win-rate gain attenuates once the unconditional sample is restricted to merits-decided cases, that the case count contracts, and that the plaintiff mix moves away from individual and non-local filers; H3 predicts that the contracted firm's Winner $\times$ Post effect appears in civil cases with no organizational party and that the firm's enterprise share rises post-procurement. The remainder of the paper reads each empirical layer as a vote on these competing mechanisms rather than as an independent robustness check."*

> *"The headline city-year point estimate on government win rate, 3.6 percentage points (Table 1, column 2), translates into roughly 14 additional government victories per treated city-year and on the order of 9,800 over the 712 treated city-years in the calibrated panel. The same coefficient moves a city at the median pre-period government-win rate to the 65th percentile of the pre-period distribution — a non-trivial reallocation in the cross-section but well short of pushing a typical city to the top of the distribution. The Callaway-Sant'Anna estimator returns a 4.9-percentage-point effect, implying about 13,300 additional victories and a percentile shift to roughly the 70th percentile. The appeal rate falls by 6.2 percentage points, equivalent to roughly 17,000 fewer appeals over the treated post-period and pulling the median pre-period city-year down to the 30th percentile of the appeal-rate distribution. The administrative case count falls by 23 cases per treated city-year (TWFE) or 91 cases per treated city-year (CS), depending on estimator. These magnitudes suggest a meaningful but bounded effect of procurement on the visible legal performance of city governments — not a regime change, but a persistent shift of roughly half of one inter-quartile distance for the typical city."*

## 7. Heterogeneity framing — political-science angles

The current heterogeneity battery (lawyer attributes, cause groups, court level) reads as econometrics-by-default. For a political-science audience the four splits below cash the same data into questions the field actually argues about. Each split should be paired with one paragraph that names the disciplinary debate it speaks to before the coefficient is reported.

### 7.1 Politically sensitive vs administrative-routine cause groups

* **Disciplinary hook.** Authoritarian-legality literature (Stern 2013; He and Su 2013; Lorentzen 2017) splits on whether legal channels function in politically sensitive disputes (where the state has the strongest incentive to manage outcomes) or only in routine administration. A null in the sensitive bucket and an effect in the routine bucket would point to "rule of law where it does not bind"; an effect concentrated in the sensitive bucket points to capacity-driven legal hardening of state defence.
* **Implementation.** Already operational in `admin_mechanism_cause_sensitivity.R` (Table 2 panel C in the proposed structure). The current implementation contrasts {expropriation, land_planning} against {labor_social, permitting_review}; for the manuscript prose the politically-sensitive bucket should be expanded to **{expropriation, land_planning, public_security}** and the routine bucket to **{labor_social, permitting_review, enforcement, generic administrative acts}**, omitting only the residual `economic_resource` group. Re-running the script with that re-categorization (a one-line change to the `HIGH_GROUPS` and `LOW_GROUPS` constants at the top of the script) and re-reading the table is enough.
* **Suggested prose.** "Authoritarian-legality work has long debated whether legal channels function in politically charged disputes or only in administrative routine (Stern 2013; He and Su 2013; Lorentzen 2017). Table 2 panel C splits the case-level effect along this margin: in the politically sensitive bucket (expropriation, land planning, public security), Treatment $\times$ Post on government win is $\beta_S$ (SE), while in the routine-administration bucket (labour and social security, permitting, enforcement, generic administrative acts) it is $\beta_R$ (SE), with an equality-test $p$-value of $p_{S\neq R}$. The pattern is consistent with [hardening of state defence in sensitive cases / a uniform capacity gain across cases / capacity-where-it-does-not-bind]."

### 7.2 Petition-pressure heterogeneity

* **Disciplinary hook.** Cai (2010), Lorentzen (2017), and Distelhorst and Hou (2017) treat administrative litigation and `xinfang` petitioning as substitutes or complements; cities under high petition pressure already face a binding constraint on bureaucratic conflict management. If procurement raises win rates more in *high*-petition cities, the headline effect reflects state-capacity investment where it is most needed (capacity hypothesis). If it raises win rates more in *low*-petition cities, it looks like co-optation or procedural display in low-pressure environments.
* **Implementation.** Not yet operational. The recipe is: split treated cities at the pre-period (years strictly before each city's first procurement year) median of `petition_rate` from `city_year_panel.csv`; estimate the headline city-year TWFE separately on the high- and low-petition subsamples, and report the equality-test $p$-value via a pooled-interaction specification. This is a 1-script add (call it `city_year_heterogeneity_petition_pressure.R`) that mirrors the structure of `admin_mechanism_cause_sensitivity.R` but at the city-year level. Pre-period median is used to keep the split exogenous to procurement.
* **Suggested prose.** "A long line of work in Chinese politics treats administrative litigation and \textit{xinfang} petitioning as competing channels for citizen redress (Cai 2010; Lorentzen 2017; Distelhorst and Hou 2017). We classify cities by their pre-procurement city-year petition rate. In the high-petition half (above the cross-city median), the procurement effect on government win is $\beta_H$ (SE); in the low-petition half it is $\beta_L$ (SE), with an equality-test $p$-value of $p_{H\neq L}$. The pattern is consistent with [a state-capacity reading: the policy moves the needle most in the cities where pressure is most binding / a co-optation reading: the policy is loudest where pressure is least binding]."

### 7.3 Cross-jurisdiction adjudication as a political-control instrument

* **Disciplinary hook.** He and Su (2013) document that local political pressure on basic-level courts is the central reason citizens lose against the state, and Liu, Wang, and Lyu (2023, *JPubE*) show that lifting cases out of local-court jurisdiction (cross-region adjudication) re-balances the win rate. Procurement should interact with this institutional shock: where the case is adjudicated by a court already insulated from the procuring city's political reach, the procurement effect should be smaller. The current cross-jurisdiction split (Appendix Table D3) is reported but the political-science framing is thin and the design is only a sub-sample comparison rather than a triple-difference.
* **Implementation.** Upgrade from sub-sample to triple-difference: estimate `government_win ~ did_treatment * cross_jurisdiction + plaintiff_is_entity + opponent_has_lawyer + ...` on the case panel, with court, year, cause-group fixed effects, clustered by city and court. Treat `cross_jurisdiction` (which is the existing data column for "case adjudicated outside basic-level local court") as the measure of insulation; the triple coefficient is the effect of procurement on government win that operates *only* through local-court adjudication. This is a 1-script add (call it `admin_triple_diff_cross_jurisdiction.R`) layered on top of the existing case panel.
* **Suggested prose.** "He and Su (2013) and Liu, Wang, and Lyu (2023) place court-level political insulation at the centre of administrative-litigation outcomes. We embed our headline procurement effect in a triple-difference: $\text{Win}_{ict} = \alpha + \beta_1 \text{D}_c \text{Post}_t + \beta_2 \text{CrossJurisdiction}_i + \beta_3 \text{D}_c \text{Post}_t \text{CrossJurisdiction}_i + \mathbf{X}_{ict}'\gamma + \xi_{c} + \xi_{t} + \xi_{g} + \varepsilon_{ict}$. The triple coefficient $\beta_3$ is the differential procurement effect on cases that are *not* adjudicated by the local basic-level court. We find $\beta_3 = ?$ (SE), so procurement [does / does not] shrink when the case is moved outside the procuring city's political reach, consistent with [a political-control reading / a representation-quality reading]."

### 7.4 Two-sided business-environment story (entity plaintiffs $\leftrightarrow$ enterprise share)

* **Disciplinary hook.** The Chinese-legal-reform literature has two sides: the citizen-vs-state docket (administrative litigation, where the entity vs individual plaintiff distinction proxies for business-environment disputes) and the firm-vs-firm docket (commercial litigation, where the procurement winner's enterprise-client share captures the supply-side response). The two have not been cleanly paired in print. Doing so makes a single-sentence claim that the same procurement intervention reweights both sides of the business-environment legal market.
* **Implementation.** No new estimation needed. The two existing tables already supply both numbers. Pair them in the manuscript prose: from `admin_plaintiff_heterogeneity_appendix_table.tex`, the entity-plaintiff Treatment $\times$ Post on government win is +0.041*** (SE 0.006), while the individual-plaintiff coefficient is +0.032*** (SE 0.005), with equality-test $p < 0.001$. From `firm_level_client_mix_mechanism_table.tex`, the Winner $\times$ Post coefficient on enterprise share is +0.040*** (SE 0.011). Display them in adjacent paragraphs of the discussion, ideally with a single side-by-side floating box.
* **Suggested prose.** "The procurement effect operates simultaneously on the two sides of the business-environment legal market. On the demand side (Appendix Table D2), the procurement-induced rise in government win is sharper in entity-plaintiff cases (+4.1 percentage points) than in individual-plaintiff cases (+3.2 percentage points), with an equality-test $p < 0.001$, suggesting that organizational adversaries face a strengthened state defence. On the supply side (Table 7), the contracted firm reorients its civil portfolio toward enterprise clients (+4.0 percentage points). Read together, the two coefficients describe a procurement intervention that both raises the cost of suing the state for organizations and pulls the contracted firm's private-client work toward those same organizations — a single state-firm reallocation that hardens the business-environment legal market on both sides."
